# DPDP Act in 3 minutes

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP) is India's first
comprehensive data-protection law. It covers every **Data Fiduciary**
(anyone who decides the purpose of processing) with personal data of
Indian residents. The DPDP Rules, 2025 and the Data Protection Board's
(DPB's) enforcement practice fill in the operational detail.

## Who does DPDP cover?

- Every business processing personal data of Indian residents — regardless
  of where the business is located.
- **Data Fiduciaries** (B2C: banks, e-commerce, fintech, clinics,
  ride-hailing…) decide the purpose and means of processing.
- **Data Processors** (SaaS vendors like ConsentShield) process on behalf
  of a Fiduciary under a contract.
- **Data Principals** are the individuals whose data is being processed —
  every Indian resident.

## Penalties

The DPB can impose up to **₹250 crore** per violation (Schedule of the
Act). The Fiduciary's board and senior management are personally
named in enforcement orders. Repeat violations compound.

<Callout tone="warn" title="Penalty is per violation, not per business">
A single breach affecting 1 million principals is typically treated as
1 violation, not 1 million. But multiple independent failures (no consent
record + no breach notification + expired purpose) each stack separately.
A 2023-era enforcement view in Indian telecom (TRAI's predecessor
regime) treated three separate obligations as three violations — DPDP is
expected to follow the same logic.
</Callout>

## Where consent artefacts come in

DPDP §6(4) requires the Fiduciary to maintain **"an audit trail of the
grant and withdrawal of consent"**. The DPB can compel production of
that trail with 48 hours' notice.

The trail isn't just a log — it's a set of artefacts:

- Who consented (identifier linked to a data principal).
- What was collected (purpose + data scope).
- When (timestamp + banner version).
- Under what notice (the exact text the principal read).
- Where from (origin + device metadata).
- What they can do next (rights-request link + withdrawal URL).

ConsentShield produces one immutable **consent artefact** per consent
decision, stores it in the customer's own R2 or S3 bucket (not
ConsentShield's database — see [stateless oracle](/docs/concepts/artefacts-vs-events)),
and exposes the whole archive via the `/v1/*` API and the dashboard.

## What DPDP does *not* require

- Localisation of every piece of data in India. (Only the DPB can notify
  cross-border transfer restrictions to specific countries.)
- A DPO for every business. (Only "Significant Data Fiduciaries" —
  threshold notified by the DPB.)
- A specific technical implementation. (The law is outcome-based — record
  the consent, honour the rights, notify the breach.)
